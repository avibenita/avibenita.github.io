 Score Distribution

      
How cases are spread on each factor — shape, skew, and overlap between factors

      

      

        

        

          
What this shows

          
Each bar counts how many cases fall in a score bin for the selected factor. You are looking at the distribution of estimated factor scores, not raw item scores. Switch factors with the dropdown, or choose Overlay both factors to compare Factor 1 and Factor 2 on the same axis with shared bins.

        

      

      

        

        

          
What to look for

          
Approximately bell-shaped — scores behave like a normal latent dimension.

          Strong skew — many cases pile up at one end; the factor may be ceiling/floor limited or the sample is homogeneous.

          Bimodal humps — possible subgroups (check the score map for clusters).

          Compare overlay mode: if two factors have very similar shapes, they may be hard to distinguish at the case level.

        

      

      

        

        

          
Scaling reminder

          
Standard factor scores cluster near 0 (SD ≈ 1). If bins sit around 100–150, read the Scaling field above — those are likely T-scores or another rescaled metric, not z-scores.

        

      

    

  


  
  

    

      
      
 Factor Score Map

      
Two factors at once — clusters, outliers, and case-level profiles

      

      

        

        

          
What this shows

          
Every point is one case positioned by its scores on the X and Y factors you select. It answers: who is high on both, low on both, or mixed? Orange outliers

          
Points turn orange when they are extreme on either axis (beyond 2 SD from that factor's mean) or by Mahalanobis distance in the 2D score space (multivariate outlier). Inspect these cases — they often drive cross-loadings or distort group comparisons.

        

      

      

        

        

          
95% ellipse & interaction

          
Toggle 95% ellipse to see the bulk of the cloud under a bivariate normal assumption — useful with oblique factors. Hover a point for Case ID and exact scores; click to highlight and scroll to that row in the export table below.

        

      

      

        

        

          
Reading patterns

          
Tight cluster — cases share similar profiles on both factors.

          Diagonal spread — factors co-vary (check Factor Correlation above).

          Separate blobs — candidate subgroups worth naming in Interpretation.

          Isolated points — unusual cases; verify data entry before dropping.

        

      

    

  


  
  

    

      
      
 Residual Correlation Matrix

      
How well the factor solution reproduces the observed correlations — cell by cell

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
What is a residual correlation?

          
A factor model claims that variables correlate because they share factors. From the loadings we can compute the correlation the model predicts for every pair of variables, then subtract it from the correlation actually observed in the data:

          residualₖₗ = rₖₗ(observed) − Σₖ λₖ₋ · λₗ₋
          
The reproduced part is the sum over factors of the two variables' loadings multiplied together (LL′). What is left over is correlation the factors cannot explain.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Reading the matrix

          
Every cell should be close to zero — that means the solution reproduces that pair's correlation well.

          ● Blue = the model under-predicts the correlation (observed > reproduced).

          ● Amber = the model over-predicts it (observed < reproduced).

          Colour intensity scales with the residual's size; the diagonal is skipped because it compares a variable with itself.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
The 0.10 rule of thumb

          
Cells with |residual| > 0.10 get a red outline. A few isolated flags are normal sampling noise. But if many cells are flagged — a common benchmark is more than ~5% of the off-diagonal cells — the model is systematically missing structure, and the summary line under the matrix turns into a warning.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
What large residuals tell you

          
1) A cluster of flagged cells among the same variables usually means one more factor is hiding there — try extracting an additional factor.

          2) A single flagged pair often signals a specific relationship (e.g. two nearly-identical items); consider dropping or combining one of them.

          3) Flags spread evenly everywhere point to a generally poor fit — revisit the extraction method or whether the data suit factor analysis.

          4) Cross-check with RMSR above: it is the average of these residuals, so a good RMSR with a few local flags is usually acceptable.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Oblique rotation caveat

          
With Promax or Oblimin, factors are allowed to correlate, and the exact reproduced matrix would need the factor correlation matrix (Φ): LΦL′. This view uses the simpler LL′ approximation, so with oblique rotations treat the residuals as indicative — small discrepancies may come from the approximation rather than a bad model.

 Anti-Image Diagnostics & MSA

      
How sampling adequacy is measured per variable, and what to do about weak items

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
What is the anti-image matrix?

          
Every correlation between two variables can be split into an image — the part predictable from all the other variables — and an anti-image, the part that is not. The anti-image correlation between two variables is their partial correlation with all other variables controlled, sign-reversed. If a genuine factor structure exists, most of each correlation should be shared (image), leaving anti-image values near zero.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
How MSA is computed

          
The Measure of Sampling Adequacy compares each variable's ordinary correlations (r) with its anti-image correlations (a) against all other variables:

          MSAₖ = Σ r² / ( Σ r² + Σ a² )
          
It approaches 1 when partial correlations are tiny (correlations are explained by shared factors) and falls toward 0 when the variable's correlations are mostly private noise. The overall KMO statistic on this page is the same computation pooled over all variables.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Kaiser's interpretation ladder

          

            ≥ 0.90 Marvelous  ·  0.80–0.89 Meritorious  ·  0.70–0.79 Middling

            0.60–0.69 Mediocre  ·  0.50–0.59 Miserable  ·  < 0.50 Unacceptable
          

          
Values below 0.50 mean the variable shares too little systematic variance with the rest of the set to be factored — it is flagged in red in the table.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Reading the anti-image matrix

          
Expand Anti-image matrix under MSA Summary to open the full matrix. The diagonal holds each variable's MSA; the off-diagonal cells are the anti-image correlations. A healthy matrix has a bright diagonal and faint off-diagonals — strongly coloured off-diagonal cells reveal variable pairs whose relationship the common factors cannot explain.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
What to do with a weak variable

          
1) Remove the variable with the lowest MSA below 0.50 and re-run the analysis.

          2) Remove one variable at a time — every removal changes all other MSA values.

          3) Re-check the overall KMO after each pass; it usually rises as weak items leave.

          4) If many variables sit below 0.50, the data may lack a common factor structure altogether — consider whether factor analysis is the right tool.

        

      

    

  


  
  

    

      
      
 Loading Highlight Rules

      
How cell colour and weight are determined in the Rotated Loadings Matrix

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Threshold (slider)

          
Only loadings with an absolute value ≥ the slider value receive colour and weight. Everything below is greyed-out at 38% opacity to suppress visual noise from near-zero loadings.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Colour intensity

          
Within the highlighted range, brightness scales linearly with how far a loading exceeds the threshold:

          intensity = ( |loading| − threshold ) / ( 1 − threshold )
          
A loading just above the threshold is faint; a loading of 1.0 is fully saturated.

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Colour direction

          

            ● Blue  =  positive loading

            ● Amber  =  negative loading


            Both use the same intensity formula — only the hue differs.
          

        

      


      

        

          
        

        

          
Bold weight

          
A loading becomes bold (700) when its intensity is ≥ 0.6 — meaning it sits in the top 40 % of the highlighted range. Below that it uses semi-bold (600).

          

            Example: threshold = 0.30, loading = 0.72

            → intensity = (0.72 − 0.30) / (1 − 0.30) = 0.60 → bold ✓
