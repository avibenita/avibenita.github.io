What is the Normal Distribution?

The normal distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped continuous distribution that plays a central role in statistical modeling, inference, and measurement. Many real-world variables are approximately normal, and many statistical methods rely on normality as a reference model.

Probability Density Function
f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) × exp( -½ ((x - μ) / σ)² )

Where:
• μ = mean (center)
• σ = standard deviation (spread)

Key Properties

Symmetry

The distribution is perfectly symmetric around the mean, so the mean, median, and mode coincide.

Bell Shape

Density peaks at the mean and declines smoothly in both directions.

Standardized Distance

Values are often interpreted through z-scores: distance from the mean in standard deviation units.

Empirical Rule

About 68% lie within ±1σ, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ.

Real-World Applications

Normal models are widely used in measurement systems, quality control, test scores, biological variables, and as an approximation basis for many inferential procedures.

The distribution is especially useful because tail areas can be mapped to thresholds, rarity, and decision rules.

Historical Background

Abraham de Moivre described the curve in the 18th century while approximating binomial probabilities. Gauss later used it in astronomical error analysis, which is why it is often called the Gaussian distribution. Laplace helped establish why normality appears so widely through early central limit arguments.
